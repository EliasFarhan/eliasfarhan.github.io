Game Programming Course --- # Welcome --- ## Course Overview Today we'll explore: * **History of Video Game Business** - From arcades to digital distribution * **The Game Economy** - How games make money * **Capital and Funding** - Understanding game development financing * **Indie Development** - Building games on your own terms This is a 2-hour journey through the business side of the industry you're learning to build for. >>> Remember: As programmers, understanding the business helps you make better technical decisions and communicate with stakeholders effectively. --- ## Why Business Matters for Programmers You might be thinking: "I just want to code games!" Here's why business knowledge matters: * **Better Design Decisions** - Technical choices impact monetization * **Career Opportunities** - Understanding business opens more paths * **Team Communication** - Speak the language of producers and executives * **Indie Success** - If you go solo, you'll need to know this The best game developers understand both code AND business. --- # Part 1: History of Video Game Business --- ## The Arcade Era (1970s-1980s) **The Birth of the Industry** * **Pong (1972)** - First commercially successful game ($8 million in 1 year) * **Space Invaders (1978)** - Generated over $3.8 billion in Japan alone * **Pac-Man (1980)** - $1 billion in quarters in first year **Business Model:** Pay-per-play. Simple, immediate revenue. Games designed for maximum quarter extraction. >>> Key insight: Arcade games were designed to be hard enough to eat quarters but fun enough to keep players coming back. This tension between difficulty and enjoyment still exists in modern monetization. --- ## The Console Revolution (1980s) **Nintendo's Game-Changing Strategy** The NES (1985) introduced: * **Licensing fees** - Third-party developers paid Nintendo to publish * **Quality control** - Nintendo Seal of Quality * **Royalty model** - Nintendo took 30% of game sales This created the platform business model that still dominates today (PlayStation, Xbox, Steam). --- ## The PC Gaming Rise (1990s) **A Different Path** * **Shareware Model** - Doom (1993) pioneered "try before you buy" * **Direct Sales** - Developers kept more revenue * **Mod Communities** - User-generated content extended game life * **CD-ROM Revolution** - Enabled larger, more complex games **Business Impact:** Lower barriers to entry, more developer freedom, but also piracy challenges. >>> The shareware model was revolutionary - id Software made more money from Doom's shareware than most retail games made at the time. --- ## The MMO Gold Rush (Late 1990s-2000s) **Subscription Economy Emerges** * **Ultima Online (1997)** - First major MMO, $10-15/month * **EverQuest (1999)** - "EverCrack" addiction, $500 million revenue * **World of Warcraft (2004)** - Peaked at 12 million subscribers ($180M/month!) **New Business Concepts:** * Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) * Virtual economies * Live operations teams * Player lifetime value (LTV) --- ## The Mobile Revolution (2007-2010) **Everything Changed Again** * **iPhone App Store (2008)** - New distribution model * **Angry Birds (2009)** - Simple gameplay, massive reach * **Freemium Model** - Free to play, pay for extras * **Candy Crush (2012)** - $1 billion revenue, mostly from 3% of players **Business Shift:** From $60 premium to free-to-play with in-app purchases. >>> The mobile revolution democratized game development - suddenly anyone could publish globally with minimal cost. But it also made discoverability the #1 challenge. --- ## Digital Distribution Era (2003-Present) **The Death of Physical Media** * **Steam (2003)** - Valve's digital storefront * **Xbox Live Arcade (2004)** - Console digital distribution * **PlayStation Network (2006)** * **Epic Games Store (2018)** - Challenging Steam with better splits **Impact:** * Lower distribution costs * Easier updates and patches * Global instant access * Platform dependency --- ## The Battle Royale & Live Service Era (2017-Present) **Games as a Service (GaaS)** * **Fortnite (2017)** - $5.8 billion in 2021 alone * **Apex Legends (2019)** - $2 billion in first year * **Genshin Impact (2020)** - $4 billion in first year **Business Model:** * Free base game * Battle passes ($10-20 per season) * Cosmetic microtransactions * Constant content updates * Community events >>> Modern live service games can generate more revenue in a month than traditional games made in their entire lifetime. But they require constant development investment. --- ## Current Landscape (2024-2026) **Where We Are Now** * **Cloud Gaming** - Xbox Cloud, GeForce Now, Amazon Luna * **Subscription Services** - Game Pass, PlayStation Plus * **NFTs & Blockchain** - Controversial, still finding place * **AI Integration** - Procedural content, NPC behavior * **Cross-platform Play** - Expected standard The industry is worth over **$200 billion globally** - larger than movies and music combined. --- # Part 2: The Economy of Games --- ## Revenue Models Overview **How Games Make Money** 1. **Premium/Paid** - Pay once, own forever ($20-70) 2. **Free-to-Play (F2P)** - Free game, optional purchases 3. **Subscription** - Monthly/yearly fees 4. **Freemium** - Free base + premium features 5. **Ad-Supported** - Free game funded by ads 6. **Hybrid Models** - Combining multiple approaches Each model has different design implications and revenue potential. --- >>> The revenue model should influence technical architecture from day one. F2P games need robust server infrastructure for live operations. Premium games might focus on polished single-player content. --- ## Premium Model Deep Dive **Traditional $60 Game** **Pros:** * Predictable revenue at launch * No ongoing monetization pressure * Clear value proposition * Better critical reception **Cons:** * All revenue upfront, limited long-tail * High price barrier reduces audience * Piracy concerns * Post-launch support needs funding **Example:** *The Last of Us Part II* - $300M development, sold 4M copies in opening weekend. --- ## Free-to-Play Economics **The Whale Economy** Player breakdown in typical F2P game: * **95% non-payers** - Never spend money * **3-4% minnows** - Spend $1-20 total * **1-2% whales** - Spend $100-1000s **Key Metrics:** * **ARPU** (Average Revenue Per User) - Total revenue / total users * **ARPPU** (Average Revenue Per Paying User) - Revenue / paying users * **Conversion Rate** - % who spend any money * **Retention** - Day 1, Day 7, Day 30 return rates --- >>> Understanding these metrics is crucial. A game with 100,000 users and 2% conversion at $50 ARPPU makes $100,000. Same game with 3% conversion makes $150,000 - a 50% revenue increase! ## Monetization Psychology **What Makes Players Spend?** * **Convenience** - Save time (energy refills, instant completions) * **Competition** - Be stronger than others * **Collection** - Complete sets, get all characters * **Expression** - Cosmetics, customization * **Progression** - Speed up advancement * **Social** - Gifts, clan features **Ethical Considerations:** * Avoid predatory practices * Transparent odds (loot boxes) * Spending limits * No pay-to-win in competitive games --- ## Battle Pass Economics **Modern Recurring Revenue** **Structure:** * Season duration: 60-90 days * Price: $8-12 * Free tier + paid tier * Levels 1-100 with rewards **Why It Works:** * Creates FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) * Encourages daily engagement * Predictable revenue * Fair feeling - you see all rewards upfront **Example:** Fortnite Battle Pass has 75%+ attach rate among active players. --- >>> Battle Passes create a psychological contract: "Play our game regularly this season and we'll reward you." This drives engagement better than random loot boxes. ## Platform Economics **The 30% Question** Traditional platform cuts: * **Steam** - 30% (drops to 25% at $10M, 20% at $50M) * **Apple/Google** - 30% (15% for small developers <$1M/year) * **PlayStation/Xbox** - 30% * **Epic Games Store** - 12% (disrupting the market) * **Itch.io** - Pay what you want (0-100%) These percentages dramatically affect profitability, especially for small studios. --- ## Regional Pricing **Global Economic Realities** Price of a $60 USD game: * **Russia** - 1999₽ (~$21 equivalent purchasing power) * **Brazil** - R$249 (~$45) * **India** - ₹3999 (~$48) * **Argentina** - $9999 (~$10 due to currency instability) * **USA** - $60 **Why Regional Pricing Matters:** * Market penetration in emerging markets * Reduces piracy * Increases total revenue * Shows cultural awareness --- >>> Steam's regional pricing can increase your global revenue by 40-60%. A $60 game priced regionally might make less per sale in some regions but sell 5-10x more copies. ## Development Cost Breakdown **Where Money Goes in Game Development** * **Salaries** - 60-75% of budget * **Marketing** - 10-30% (often equals development cost for AAA) * **Middleware/Licensing** - 5-10% (engines, tools, music) * **Office/Operations** - 5-10% * **Testing/QA** - 5-8% **Scale:** * Indie game - $50K - $500K * AA game - $2M - $20M * AAA game - $50M - $200M+ --- ## Marketing Economics **The Discoverability Challenge** * **Steam releases per day** - 30-40 games * **Mobile apps per day** - 3,000+ games * **Marketing spend for AAA** - Often equals development budget **Cost-Effective Marketing:** * Influencer/streamer outreach * Community building * Early access/beta programs * Game festivals (PAX, GDC, indie events) * Social media presence **One truth:** The best game ever made will fail if no one knows it exists. --- >>> Fortnite spent $100M on development and reportedly $100M+ on marketing in the first year. That initial push created the cultural phenomenon. But indie games can succeed with clever, targeted marketing on tiny budgets. # Part 3: How Capital Works --- ## Bootstrapping **Self-Funding Your Game** **Advantages:** * Full creative control * Keep 100% equity * No pressure for specific milestones * Freedom to pivot **Challenges:** * Limited budget restricts scope * Personal financial risk * Slower development * May need side job **Success Stories:** *Stardew Valley* (1 person, 4 years), *Undertale* ($50K Kickstarter + savings) --- >>> Most indie developers bootstrap their first game while working another job. It's slow and hard, but you maintain complete control and learn the full development cycle. ## Publisher Funding **Traditional Game Publishing** **Publisher Provides:** * Development funding * Marketing budget * QA testing * Distribution * Localization **Publisher Takes:** * 30-70% of revenue (often 50-70%) * IP rights (sometimes) * Creative control (varies) * Recoupment before developer sees money --- ## Publisher Deal Structures **Understanding the Terms** **Advance Against Royalty:** * Publisher gives you $500K upfront * You owe that back from your share of revenue * Only get royalties after recoupment * Example: At 30% developer share, need $1.67M revenue to recoup **Profit Share:** * No advance, split profits 50/50 * Less risk, less upfront cash * Start earning immediately **Work-for-Hire:** * Fixed payment, publisher owns IP * Like consulting, guaranteed money --- >>> Read every contract carefully! Many developers never see royalties because "marketing costs" or "platform fees" are deducted before calculating their share. Lawyer up! ## Venture Capital (VC) **Investment for Growth** **What VCs Look For:** * Large addressable market * Scalable business model * Strong team * Competitive advantage * Path to 10x return **Typical Terms:** * Investment: $500K - $5M (seed/Series A) * Equity: 15-25% * Board seat * Liquidation preferences * Anti-dilution protection --- ## VC: The Good and Bad **Advantages:** * Significant capital for growth * Industry connections * Business expertise * Validation **Disadvantages:** * Give up equity and control * Pressure for massive growth * Exit expectations (acquisition or IPO) * Misaligned incentives (VC wants 10x, you want sustainable business) **Reality:** Only ~1% of game studios get VC funding. It's not right for everyone. --- >>> VCs invest in a portfolio expecting 9/10 companies to fail, but 1/10 to return 100x. This creates pressure to "go big or go home" rather than building a sustainable business. ## Grants and Subsidies **Government and Foundation Support** **Examples:** * **Canada Media Fund** - Up to $750K for Canadian studios * **UK Games Fund** - £25K prototyping grants * **Epic MegaGrants** - $5K to $500K, no strings attached * **Creative Europe** - EU game development support * **Regional Film Commissions** - Often fund games too **Benefits:** * Non-dilutive (keep your equity!) * Often support experimental/artistic games * Relatively small amounts but helpful --- ## Crowdfunding **Community-Funded Development** **Platforms:** * **Kickstarter** - All-or-nothing funding * **Indiegogo** - Keep what you raise * **Patreon** - Ongoing support * **Fig** - Combines crowdfunding with investment **Success Factors:** * Strong prototype/vertical slice * Compelling story and vision * Engaged community * Transparent communication * Reasonable funding goal **Reality Check:** Only ~35% of game Kickstarters succeed. --- >>> Kickstarter isn't free money - it's pre-orders with accountability. You're promising to deliver, and backers expect results. Failed projects damage your reputation permanently. ## Crowdfunding: Case Studies **Successes:** * **Star Citizen** - $600M+ (ongoing) * **Pillars of Eternity** - $4M (asked $1.1M) * **Shovel Knight** - $311K (asked $75K) **Failures:** * **Mighty No. 9** - Funded $4M, delivered mediocre game * **Yogventures** - $567K, never delivered **Key Lesson:** Crowdfunding is validation and funding, but you still need to build a great game. --- ## Early Access Model **Pay to Play While Developing** **How It Works:** * Release unfinished but playable game * Charge reduced price ($10-30) * Regular updates based on feedback * Full launch later at full price **Advantages:** * Revenue during development * Community feedback * Marketing/awareness building * Validates market interest **Risks:** * First impressions matter - release too early and damage reputation * Feature creep from community * Burnout from public scrutiny --- >>> Early Access success stories like Minecraft, Valheim, and Hades show the model works. But for every success, there are hundreds of abandoned Early Access games that killed their studio's reputation. ## Revenue Sharing & Royalties **Alternative Funding Models** **Revenue Share Partnership:** * Team members work for % of revenue instead of salary * Common in indie teams * Everyone invested in success **Example:** * 4-person team, each gets 25% * Game makes $400K * Each person gets $100K **Risks:** * No guaranteed income * Relationship strain if game fails * Disputes over contribution vs. percentage --- ## Understanding Equity **Ownership in Your Studio** **Key Concepts:** * **Equity** - Ownership percentage * **Dilution** - Your % goes down as you raise money * **Vesting** - Earning equity over time (typically 4 years) * **Cliff** - Period before vesting starts (often 1 year) **Example:** * You own 100% initially * Raise $1M at $4M valuation → 20% dilution, you own 80% * Raise another $2M at $8M valuation → 20% dilution, you own 64% Each funding round dilutes, but increases total pie value. --- >>> Owning 50% of a $10M company is better than owning 100% of a $1M company. Don't be afraid of dilution if it helps you build something bigger. # Part 4: Indie Game Development --- ## What is "Indie"? **Defining Independent Development** Originally meant: * Financially independent (self-funded or small funding) * Creatively independent (no publisher) * Small team (1-15 people) **Modern Reality:** * Some "indies" have $2M budgets * Some work with publishers * The spirit of independence matters more than strict definition **Core Principle:** Creative freedom and ownership over commercial success. --- >>> "Indie" is more about mindset than team size. It's about making the game you want to make, not what focus groups say will sell. ## The Indie Advantage **Why Go Independent?** **Creative Freedom:** * Make weird, experimental games * Target niche audiences * Take artistic risks * Iterate without oversight **Financial Potential:** * Keep more revenue * Sustainable small successes possible * Platforms enable global distribution * Lower overhead means lower break-even **Learning:** * Wear all hats * Understand full pipeline * Build diverse skills --- ## The Indie Reality Check **Harsh Truths** * **Most indie games lose money** - Only ~25% make any profit * **Takes longer than planned** - Multiply your estimate by 2-3x * **Marketing is essential** - You must promote actively * **Burnout is real** - 60-80 hour weeks are common * **Day job likely needed** - Few can afford full-time development initially **But:** The successful ones can be life-changing, both creatively and financially. --- >>> For every Stardew Valley making $24M, there are 10,000 indie games that made $5,000. Go in with eyes open, but don't let statistics kill your dreams. ## Scope Management **The #1 Killer of Indie Projects** **Common Mistakes:** * "We'll build an MMO with 5 people!" * "We'll make this in 6 months!" (2 years later...) * "Just add this one more feature..." **Survival Strategies:** * Start small - make your 10th game idea, not your dream game * Minimum Viable Game concept * Vertical slice first * Cut features ruthlessly * Time-box development **Example:** *Hollow Knight* started as a smaller game, only expanded after early success. --- ## Solo Development **The One-Person Studio** **Advantages:** * Complete creative control * Keep 100% revenue * Simple decision making * Flexible schedule **Challenges:** * Everything falls on you (code, art, sound, marketing, business) * Loneliness and isolation * Skill gaps * Slower progress * No one to catch mistakes **Tools That Help:** Unity/Godot, Aseprite, BFXR, free music assets, contractor platforms --- >>> Solo development is appealing but brutally hard. Most successful solo devs have significant experience and use heavy asset reuse or simple art styles. Don't solo dev your first serious game. ## Small Team Dynamics **2-10 Person Studios** **Ideal Team Composition:** * 1-2 Programmers * 1-2 Artists * 1 Designer (often overlaps with programmer) * 0.5 Audio (contractor) * 0.5 Marketing/Community (often founder) **Making It Work:** * Clear roles and ownership * Written agreements (even with friends!) * Regular milestones * Communication tools * Shared vision **Reality:** Most successful indies are 2-4 person teams. --- ## Revenue Expectations **What Can Indie Games Actually Make?** **Steam Release Statistics (2023):** * **Bottom 50%** - Less than $5,000 total * **Top 25%** - $50,000+ * **Top 10%** - $250,000+ * **Top 1%** - $1M+ **Mobile (even harder):** * **90% of apps** - Less than $1,000/month * **Top 10%** - $5,000+/month **Breakout Hits:** * Stardew Valley - $24M+ * Among Us - $100M+ (took 2 years to explode) * Vampire Survivors - $100M+ (1 person) --- >>> Plan for the median outcome, not the lottery. If your game makes $50K, does that sustain your studio? What's your break-even point? ## Marketing for Indies **You Can't Ignore This** **The Reality:** * Development: 50% of your time * Marketing: 50% of your time **Effective Indie Marketing:** * **Build Community Early** - Twitter, Discord, Reddit * **GIFs/Videos** - Show gameplay constantly * **Streamer Outreach** - Send keys to relevant creators * **Demo/Prologue** - Let people try it free * **Festivals** - Steam Next Fest, PAX, local events * **Wishlists** - Critical on Steam (aim for 10K+) * **Dev Logs** - Document your journey --- >>> Steam's algorithm heavily weighs wishlists. A game with 50K wishlists will get prominent placement on launch day. Start building wishlists 6-12 months before release. ## When to Launch **Timing Your Release** **Avoid:** * Holiday season (Nov-Dec) - AAA saturation * Major game releases in your genre * Summer drought (unless you're the only game) **Good Times:** * January-March (post-holiday, people want new games) * September (back to routine) * Your game festival demo success **Platform Events:** * Steam Next Fest (February, June, October) * Platform sales events * Themed sale events matching your genre --- ## Sustainability Models **Making Indie Development Last** **Portfolio Approach:** * Build 3-4 smaller games instead of 1 big one * Each game has higher success chance * Build audience across titles * Lessons learned compound **Service Model:** * Consulting/contract work * Asset store sales * Tutorials/courses * Patreon/sponsorships **Slow Burn:** * Keep day job * Develop nights/weekends * No pressure for immediate success * Build skills steadily --- >>> Many successful indie studios started with contract work to fund their passion projects. There's no shame in having a sustainable business model that includes non-game revenue. ## Community Building **Your Most Valuable Asset** **Why Community Matters:** * Free playtesting * Marketing amplification * Direct feedback * Emotional support * Revenue (wishlists/purchases) **Building Your Community:** * **Discord Server** - Your home base * **Social Media** - Twitter for devs, TikTok for younger audiences * **Reddit** - r/gamedev, genre-specific subs * **Streaming** - Show development live * **Newsletter** - Own your communication channel **Golden Rule:** Give value before asking. Share knowledge, help others, be genuine. --- >>> The developers who succeed are often generous with their knowledge. Your community becomes your support network, QA team, and marketing force. ## Tools and Costs **Budgeting Your Indie Game** **Free Tools:** * Godot (engine) * Blender (3D) * GIMP/Krita (2D) * Audacity (audio) * DaVinci Resolve (video editing) **Paid but Worthwhile:** * Unity ($185/year for Plus, free for small revenue) * Unreal (free, 5% royalty after $1M) * Aseprite ($20, pixel art) * Substance Painter ($20/month, texturing) **Other Costs:** * App store fees ($100/year Apple, $25 one-time Google) * Domain/web hosting ($50/year) * Marketing budget ($500-5000) **Total to launch:** $500-3000 as absolute minimum. --- >>> You can make a game with $0 budget using free tools. But investing a few hundred dollars in good tools and marketing can dramatically improve your chances. ## Legal Basics **Protecting Yourself** **Essential Documents:** * **LLC Formation** - Protect personal assets ($100-500) * **Contracts** - For any collaborators or contractors * **IP Assignment** - Ensure you own all assets * **Privacy Policy** - Required for data collection * **Terms of Service** - Define user agreements **Tax Considerations:** * Business expenses are deductible * Set aside 25-30% for taxes * Quarterly estimated taxes if profitable * Track ALL expenses **When to Get a Lawyer:** Before signing any contract or forming a partnership. --- ## Success Stories **Learning from Indie Hits** **Stardew Valley (Eric Barone):** * 4 years solo development * No marketing budget * Pixel art kept scope manageable * Launched at $15, sold 20M+ copies **Hollow Knight (Team Cherry):** * 3-person core team * Kickstarter for $57K * 2.5 years development * Launched at $15, sold 5M+ copies * Constant free updates built loyalty **Vampire Survivors (Luca Galante):** * Solo developer * $3 price point * Launched early access * Minimal graphics, focus on gameplay * Made $100M+ --- >>> What do these share? Reasonable scope, clear vision, strong core gameplay loop, and developers who finished the game. ## Common Indie Failures **Learn from Mistakes** **Scope Creep:** * Started small, kept adding features * Never finished * Burned out **No Marketing:** * "Build it and they will come" * Launch day: 12 sales * Surprised no one knew about it **Wrong Platform:** * Console-quality game on mobile * Mobile game on PC * Pricing mismatch **Team Collapse:** * No written agreements * Money disputes * Creative differences * Someone quits mid-project --- >>> Every failed indie game teaches a lesson. Read postmortems (gamasutra.com has hundreds). Learn from others' expensive mistakes. ## Your Indie Journey **Getting Started Today** **Week 1:** Pick your first tiny project (1 week scope) * One mechanic * Simple art * Finish it **Month 1:** Build 4 tiny games * Learn full pipeline * Get comfortable shipping **Month 6:** Start your "real" first indie game * 3-6 month scope * Use lessons learned * Start marketing early **Year 1:** Launch, learn, iterate * Reflect on what worked * Build community * Plan next project --- >>> The biggest difference between successful indie devs and aspiring ones? Successful ones ship games. Start small, finish projects, build momentum. # Conclusion --- ## Key Takeaways **Business of Games in One Slide:** * **History teaches us** - Business models constantly evolve, adapt or die * **Economy is complex** - Multiple revenue streams, understand your metrics * **Capital has costs** - Every funding source has tradeoffs * **Indie is viable** - But requires business savvy, not just game dev skills **Most Important:** Understanding business makes you a better developer and increases your chances of success, whether indie or AAA. --- ## Resources for Further Learning **Books:** * "Blood, Sweat, and Pixels" by Jason Schreier * "The Art of Game Design" by Jesse Schell * "Crossing the Chasm" by Geoffrey Moore (general startup advice) **Websites/Blogs:** * Gamasutra/Game Developer * How to Market a Game (Chris Zukowski) * GDC Vault talks * Derek Yu's "Make Games" blog **Communities:** * r/gamedev * r/indiegamedev * IGDA chapters * Local game dev meetups --- ## Action Items **For Your Next Project:** 1.
